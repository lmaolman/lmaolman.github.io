- Mouthpiece -

Conception and Inspiration

My background is, first and foremost, being a composer of contemporary concert music. Music which is meant to be listened to in-concert, usually in a concert hall, performed by live musicians. There is an inherent power dynamic which must be navigated by anyone in this field. As a composer, at least in the most traditional sense of the word, you are writing a piece of music, for which you expect musicians to read, practice, and perform. The composer (again, traditional sense) does not create a single sound which the audience hears. Like the coach of a football team, the composer often gets much credit despite not taking a single metaphorical tackle.

So I wanted to explore this idea of leeching off of someone’s medium. As a composer, I leech off of instrumentalists and performers, so how could I preserve this idea, this commentary? I figured, what if this parasitic relationship was at the forefront, where the audience could see it happening right in front of their eyes. That was the fundamental idea behind Mouthpiece. A technically simple sonic manipulation device, designed not to create sound on its own, but become the mouthpiece through which others’ sound is appropriated.

A glove-based system seemed most intuitive to me, from a pure expression and theatrical perspective. There is of course a lot of precedent as well, see Imogen Heap for the biggest inspiration. But honestly, the project didn’t really have any specific project or system in mind, just the aforementioned artistic goal.

Instrument Behavior

The instrument's hardware is rather simple. It consists of 3 buttons and an ultrasonic distance sensor, mounted to two gloves. The Arduino Uno microcontroller is mounted the the left arm, which is then connected to the computer. A contact microphone (or any microphone which will minimally feedback) is mounted to any instrumnet, preferrably one which can be muted to limit the natural acoustic emission of it's sound.

The patch consists of three fundamental effect subpatches. The first is a feedback loop, which when triggered (button 1), takes a small recording of the sound currently happening, then at certain intervals determined by the distance sensor reading, outputs that sound again at a lower volume before feeding that back into the loop, to be played again. This will cause a disintegration of the characteristics of the sound. The second is a tremolo patch, which when activated (button 1) performs a periodic amplitude adjustment, again the speed of which determined by the distance sensor. The effect is one of tremolo, or "warbling" of the sound. The final effect is a series of spectral freeze patches. These, when triggered (button 1), store the sound currently being input by the micropone, then can be played back by a different button (button 2). These are stacked through various buffers (technically jit matrices), and can be played back simaltanously. The other effects can also be used on these stored spectral freeze sounds too, not just the raw microphone input. The different effects are changed through a one second press of the remaining button (button 3). The distance sensor data is also smoothed via EMA smoothing, then limited to a max value of 50.
